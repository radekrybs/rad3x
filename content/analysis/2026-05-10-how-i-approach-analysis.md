When I sit down to analyze something — a market, a dataset, a decision — I try to
follow the same loop. It keeps me from fooling myself.

## 1. State the question precisely

A vague question produces a vague answer. "Is this a good market?" becomes
"How many people have this problem badly enough to pay, and how is that number
changing?"

## 2. Decide what would change my mind

Before looking at any data, I write down what evidence would push me toward
*yes* and what would push me toward *no*. This is the single most important step
— it's the antidote to confirmation bias.

| Signal | Bull case | Bear case |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Demand | Growing search volume | Flat or declining |
| Willingness to pay | Existing paid alternatives | Everything is free |
| Competition | Fragmented, sleepy incumbents | One dominant, fast player |

## 3. Gather, then weigh

Collect the evidence, but don't treat every data point equally. A single
conversation with a real user often outweighs a glossy industry report.

## 4. Write the conclusion as a bet

I end every analysis with an explicit, falsifiable claim and a confidence level.
"I think X, at roughly 70% confidence, and here's what would change that."

That's it. The structure is boring on purpose — boring is repeatable.
